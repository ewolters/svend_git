01 Bayesian Methods — The Foundation
Tier 1

Bayesian Process Capability (Bayesian Cpk)

Capability analysis without the 1.5σ assumption

Replace the made-up 1.5 sigma shift with a posterior distribution of the actual process shift, estimated from data. Every Cpk calculation includes a credible interval. Prior knowledge from similar processes bootstraps new product introductions with limited data. Academic literature validated since 2005 (Pearn & Wu, Maiti 2012). Zero commercial implementation anywhere.

BenefitsProcess-specific shift estimates. Credible intervals around Cpk itself. Prior knowledge from similar processes transfers. Small-sample pharma validation is killer use case.
RisksPractitioners trained on classical Cpk need education. Prior specification requires some domain knowledge. Certification bodies still teach classical methods.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★★★ SEO: Wide Open

Bayesian SPC with Prior Knowledge

Useful control limits from day one, not after 25 subgroups

New production line starts with informative priors from similar processes. Control chart has meaningful limits immediately instead of running blind for weeks. Priors update with incoming data via standard Bayesian updating. Each production run makes the model better. Perfectly aligned with Svend's data-appreciates-over-time philosophy.

BenefitsEliminates the dangerous "blind production" period at startup. Priors accumulate across product families. Compounding value — switching cost rises with every batch. Particularly valuable for short-run manufacturing and pharma.
RisksMisspecified priors could mislead. Need clear UI showing prior vs. data influence. Regulatory acceptance uncertain for some industries.

Tier 1

Bayesian Experimental Design (Sequential DOE)

Run 3 experiments, update beliefs, design the optimal 4th

Bayesian Optimization replaces classical factorial/response surface DOE with adaptive experimentation. Uses Gaussian Process surrogate models to predict outcomes, then selects the most informative next experiment via acquisition functions (Expected Improvement, UCB). Bioprocess research shows BO requires roughly 50% fewer experiments than classical DOE for equivalent optimization (Claes et al. 2024). Merck KGaA (BayBE), Merck & Co (Obsidian), and Novo Nordisk (ProcessOptimizer) have built internal tools — but no commercial quality platform offers this. Fraunhofer IPT published comprehensive benchmarking in Nov 2025.

Benefits50-69% fewer experiments than classical DOE. Every experiment maximally informative. Handles noisy, expensive, multi-objective problems. GP surrogate provides uncertainty at every prediction point. Converges faster on expensive validation runs.
RisksGP kernel selection requires expertise (Matérn-ARD recommended for manufacturing). Noise degrades performance — requires precise sensors. Practitioners trained on classical DOE need re-education. Multi-objective version (MOBO) adds complexity.
Effort: High Lock-in: ★★★★☆ SEO: Moderate

Tier 2

Bayesian Change Point Detection

"94% probability the mean shifted between observations 43 and 46"

Instead of binary in-control / out-of-control alarms, provide a posterior probability distribution over when and how much a process shifted. Outputs the most likely new mean, the credible interval for the changepoint location, and the posterior probability of shift. Feeds directly into RCA Challenge — the system triggers investigation with richer starting information than a single alarm point.

BenefitsMuch richer information for root cause investigation. Quantifies magnitude of shift, not just presence. Can detect multiple changepoints. Natural integration with probabilistic SPC.
RisksComputationally heavier than CUSUM/EWMA. Results harder to explain to shop-floor operators. May require simplification layer for UI.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★☆☆ SEO: Niche

Tier 2

Probabilistic Process Monitoring

"Probability of shift is 73% and rising" — not binary alarms

Replace the traditional Shewhart alarm (point crosses line = alarm) with a continuous probability that accumulates evidence of process change. Actionable before a classical alarm fires. Can incorporate multiple evidence streams — SPC signals, measurement system health, environmental data. The probability curve itself becomes the operator's dashboard — intuitive, actionable, and fundamentally different from anything in Minitab.

BenefitsOperators act on accumulating probability, not single-point alarms. Reduces both false alarms and missed shifts. Integrates naturally with risk-based quality systems. Ties into RCA trigger logic.
RisksNew mental model for operators trained on control chart rules. Needs careful calibration of probability thresholds per process. Potential for alarm fatigue if threshold set too low.

New Statistics (Effect Sizes + Estimation Thinking)

Cumming's framework, everywhere in the platform

Effect sizes and confidence intervals as primary outputs, not accessories to p-values. APA changed reporting guidelines. Journals increasingly require effect sizes. University programs shifting curricula. JASP and jamovi have academic implementations, but zero commercial quality/manufacturing tools have adopted this. Svend's entire statistical output layer should default to estimation thinking — effect sizes front and center, CIs always visible, p-values available but secondary.

BenefitsAligns with where academia is heading — graduates expect this. Answers "how much?" not just "is it different?" More actionable for practitioners. Differentiates every statistical output from competitors.
RisksCertification exams still teach NHST. Some practitioners will want p-values front-and-center. Need to support both paradigms without confusing either audience.

Tier 1

Anytime-Valid Inference (E-values / SAFE Testing)

Peek at your data whenever you want — conclusions stay valid

The hottest development in statistical theory. E-values and confidence sequences let you analyze data continuously as it arrives without inflating error rates. Classical tests break if you "peek" — anytime-valid methods don't. Spotify and GrowthBook use this for A/B testing. Grünwald et al. published the definitive paper in JRSS-B (Nov 2024). For manufacturing: run your experiment, check daily, stop when you have enough evidence, reallocate resources immediately. Nobody has brought this to quality engineering.

BenefitsEliminates the "wait for 25 subgroups" ritual. Experiments conclude faster — saves real money. Mathematically rigorous (not a hack). Combines Bayesian interpretability with frequentist error guarantees. Unites two philosophical camps.
RisksConfidence sequences are wider than fixed-sample CIs at any given point. Theory is new — few practitioners understand it yet. Implementation needs careful tuning of the rho parameter. Regulatory acceptance unknown.
Effort: High Lock-in: ★★★★☆ SEO: Wide Open

Tier 1

Conformal Prediction for Process Monitoring

Distribution-free control charts with guaranteed coverage

A December 2025 paper (arxiv 2512.23602) proposes "Conformal-Enhanced Control Charts" — distribution-free, model-agnostic, works on any data shape without normality assumptions. Two novel applications: prediction intervals that adapt width to process uncertainty ("uncertainty spikes" as leading indicators), and a p-value chart that reframes multivariate monitoring as anomaly detection. The paper is two months old. No commercial implementation exists. This is brand new.

BenefitsNo normality assumption required — works on any data distribution. "Uncertainty spikes" warn before control limits are breached. Model-agnostic — wraps any prediction model. Finite-sample guarantees, not asymptotic. Multivariate monitoring becomes simple p-value chart.
RisksVery new — no practitioner awareness yet. Intervals can be conservative (wider than necessary). Requires calibration data set. Academic framing needs translation to operator language.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★☆☆ SEO: Wide Open

Tier 3

Conformal Prediction Intervals on ML Outputs

Every ML prediction wrapped in a guaranteed coverage interval

Every time the ML Hub makes a prediction, wrap it in a conformal interval guaranteed to contain the true value with user-specified probability (e.g. 90%). No distributional assumptions. Works on any model — random forests, gradient boosting, anything. "Model predicts optimal temp 185°C, 90% interval [181, 189]." No quality ML tool provides this.

BenefitsUncertainty quantification for free on any model. Distribution-free guarantees. Intervals adapt — wider when model is uncertain, tighter when confident. Simple to explain to practitioners.
RisksRequires a held-out calibration set (reduces training data). Marginal coverage ≠ conditional coverage — intervals may not be well-calibrated for subgroups. Conservative with small calibration sets.
Effort: Low Lock-in: ★★☆☆☆ SEO: Moderate

03 Local ML — Practical Problem-Solving
Tier 2

Gaussian Process Regression as Surrogate Model

The probabilistic "digital twin" of your process

GPs serve as the engine underneath Bayesian DOE and Bayesian Optimization, but they're also standalone powerful: fit a GP to your process data and you get a prediction plus uncertainty at every point in the parameter space. Enables "what-if" exploration without running experiments. The GP is the underlying workhorse for most methods in this report — building a robust GP implementation is foundational infrastructure.

BenefitsPrediction + uncertainty at every point. Natural interpolator — passes through observed data. Foundation for BO, DOE, process optimization. Elegant nonparametric regression without assumptions about functional form.
RisksScales poorly with large datasets (O(n³) computation). Kernel selection non-trivial. Can overfit with small data and wrong kernel. Not a user-facing feature — infrastructure component.

Tier 2

Gradient Boosted Trees with SHAP Explanations

Best-in-class tabular prediction + "why did it predict that?"

XGBoost/LightGBM remain the best non-neural models for structured/tabular data (which is what manufacturing data is). Combined with SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations), each prediction comes with a decomposition of which features drove it. For quality prediction: "This batch is predicted defective because nozzle temperature was 3° high and humidity was elevated." Explainability is mandatory for regulated industries.

BenefitsState-of-art tabular performance. SHAP provides per-prediction explanations. Fast to train, fast to predict. Handles missing data, mixed types. Practitioners can understand feature importance plots.
RisksSHAP assumes feature independence — can misattribute when features are correlated (major issue for manufacturing data). SHAP shows correlation, NOT causation. Can mislead practitioners into treating symptoms as root causes. Need Causal SHAP (see Section 4) to address this.
Effort: Low Lock-in: ★★☆☆☆ SEO: Competitive

Tier 2

Survival Analysis for Reliability & Predictive Maintenance

How long until this component fails — with uncertainty

Goes beyond simple Weibull fits (what Minitab offers) to modern survival models: Random Survival Forests, Cox PH with time-varying covariates, and Bayesian survival models with priors from similar equipment families. Canon Production Printing (2025) used survival models to predict printhead lifespan. Handles censored data naturally (you know some units haven't failed yet). Critical for warranty prediction, preventive maintenance scheduling, and equipment lifecycle management.

BenefitsHandles censored data (units still running). Bayesian survival allows prior knowledge transfer. Links directly to warranty cost prediction. Enables condition-based maintenance scheduling. Minitab only offers basic parametric survival — modernizing this is differentiating.
RisksRequires time-to-event data which many companies don't collect well. Censoring types must be modeled correctly. Random Survival Forests are less interpretable than parametric models. Separate from core quality analytics — scope creep risk.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★☆☆ SEO: Moderate

Tier 3

Multi-Armed Bandits for Adaptive Quality Decisions

Explore vs. exploit — optimized in real time

Thompson Sampling and UCB algorithms for sequential decision-making where you balance exploring alternatives against exploiting the best known option. Applications: adaptive inspection sampling (inspect more from suspect sources, less from proven ones), real-time process parameter tuning, A/B testing of process improvements. The bandit framework is the mathematical formalization of "try things intelligently."

BenefitsOptimal exploration/exploitation tradeoff. Adapts sampling intensity to risk. Thompson Sampling is Bayesian — aligns with platform philosophy. Directly applicable to inspection optimization.
RisksAbstract concept — practitioners won't understand "bandit" framing. Needs to be embedded inside familiar workflows, not exposed directly. Reward definition is critical and non-trivial. Switching costs between "arms" add complexity.

04 Causal Inference — Beyond Correlation
Tier 1

Causal Discovery (DAG-Based RCA)

Distinguish "A causes B" from "A and B share a cause"

Use the PC algorithm, FCI, or LiNGAM to automatically discover causal structure from observational process data. Output is a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) showing which variables cause which. This transforms root cause analysis from opinion-based to evidence-based. A 2024 European patent (EP4310618B1) describes deep learning causal inference for industrial process RCA. Salesforce released CausalAI library supporting both tabular and time-series data. A June 2025 Medium analysis demonstrated that SHAP misattributes root causes by giving importance to symptoms, while causal ML correctly identifies true drivers.

BenefitsDistinguishes causes from symptoms — the fundamental RCA problem. Automated graph construction from process data. Enables counterfactual reasoning ("what if we changed X?"). Pairs perfectly with RCA Challenge feature. Published patent validates commercial viability.
RisksCausal discovery from observational data has known limitations (hidden confounders). Requires sufficient data and variable coverage. PC algorithm assumes no hidden variables (use FCI if not true). Domain expert validation still needed. Can produce incorrect graphs with insufficient data.
Effort: High Lock-in: ★★★★★ SEO: Wide Open
Svend Fit

Tier 2

Causal SHAP

Feature attribution that respects causal structure

Published August 2025 (arxiv 2509.00846). Integrates PC algorithm causal discovery with SHAP values via the IDA algorithm for causal strength quantification. When features are correlated (which they always are in manufacturing), standard SHAP misattributes — Causal SHAP reduces attribution for features that are merely correlated with the target. This is the bridge between explainable ML and actual causal understanding.

BenefitsFixes SHAP's biggest flaw for manufacturing data. Preserves SHAP's theoretical properties while adding causal awareness. Directly applicable to quality prediction models. Better insertion scores on real-world datasets even without known causal graph.
RisksVery new (Aug 2025). Relies on PC algorithm accuracy for causal graph. Computational cost higher than standard SHAP. Doesn't yet handle hidden variables (future work with FCI). Two-month-old paper — not yet battle-tested.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★☆☆ SEO: Wide Open

Tier 3

Counterfactual Simulation ("What-If" Engine)

"If we had set temperature to 180° instead of 190°, would the defect have occurred?"

Once you have a causal model (DAG + structural equations), you can answer counterfactual questions. This is Pearl's "rung three" of the causal ladder — the highest level of causal reasoning. A 2025 paper proposes using a pre-trained causal foundation model as a "what-if" engine for prescriptive maintenance, estimating the causal effect of each potential intervention on OEE. Converts RCA from "what happened" to "what should we do."

BenefitsTransforms RCA from diagnostic to prescriptive. Enables intervention simulation without physical experiments. Ranks corrective actions by estimated causal effect. Natural language outputs possible ("changing X would reduce defects by Y%").
RisksRequires well-specified structural causal model. Sensitive to model misspecification. Counterfactual claims are inherently unverifiable for individual cases. Significant implementation complexity. Frontier research, not production-ready.
Effort: Very High Lock-in: ★★★★★ SEO: Niche

05 Adaptive Monitoring & Drift Detection
Tier 2

Concept Drift Detection for Model Health

Your ML model is decaying — the system tells you before it matters

Manufacturing processes change — new raw materials, tool wear, seasonal effects, operator turnover. Any ML model trained on historical data degrades as the process drifts. Concept drift detection monitors the statistical properties of incoming data and model predictions, alerting when the model's assumptions no longer hold. Methods include ADWIN (adaptive windowing), DDM (drift detection method), and Bayesian uncertainty monitoring. A 2024 Frontiers paper provides the first comprehensive survey of unsupervised drift detection for manufacturing and quality control.

BenefitsPrevents silent model degradation. Automates model health monitoring. Can trigger retraining automatically. Essential for any deployed ML model. Differentiates Svend's ML Hub as "self-aware" — models know when they're wrong.
RisksFalse alarms (detecting drift when none occurred). Needs threshold tuning per model. Real-time monitoring adds infrastructure complexity. Some drift is benign (seasonal) — need to distinguish from harmful drift.
Effort: Medium Lock-in: ★★★☆☆ SEO: Low
Svend Fit

Tier 3

Online Learning / Incremental Model Updates

Models learn from every new batch, not just historical training data

Instead of static models retrained periodically, online learning updates models incrementally with each new observation. Relevant for SPC models, quality prediction, and process optimization where the process itself evolves. River (Python library) provides streaming ML. Combined with drift detection, creates a self-adapting system that stays current automatically.

BenefitsModels never go stale. Adapts to gradual process changes automatically. Lighter than full retraining. Natural fit for streaming manufacturing data. Combined with Bayesian updating, extremely powerful.
RisksCatastrophic forgetting — model can lose knowledge of rare events. Adversarial data can corrupt the model over time. Needs careful validation to ensure updates improve, not degrade. More complex infrastructure than batch training.
Effort: High Lock-in: ★★★★☆ SEO: None

Anomaly Detection via Isolation Forest / LOF

Find the weird data points — unsupervised, no labels needed

Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor detect anomalous observations in multivariate process data without requiring labeled examples of defects. Useful for incoming material inspection, final product screening, and sensor data monitoring. Well-established methods (scikit-learn native) but the quality engineering framing and integration with SPC workflow is differentating.

BenefitsNo labeled data required. Detects multivariate anomalies that univariate charts miss. Fast to train and score. Well-understood, battle-tested algorithms.
RisksWell-known methods — no competitive moat. Threshold setting is subjective. High-dimensional data can confuse distance-based methods. Not novel enough to be a differentiator.

Tier 2

Measurement System Bayesian Updating (MSA 2.0)

Your measurement system degrades over time — track it probabilistically

Classical Gage R&R is a one-time study. In reality, measurement systems drift — calibration degrades, sensors age, operators change. Bayesian MSA maintains a running posterior on measurement system health, updating with each calibration check. When the posterior probability of adequate measurement capability drops below threshold, the system alerts. Links measurement health to decision confidence — if your gage is degraded, your Cpk has wider uncertainty.

BenefitsContinuous MSA instead of periodic studies. Links measurement uncertainty to decision confidence. Priors from similar instruments transfer. Alerts before measurement system fails audit. Unique — nobody has this.
RisksRequires regular calibration data input (operational discipline). MSA is already unpopular with practitioners — adding complexity risks avoidance. Need dead-simple UI. Niche appeal — not a headline feature.

Tier 3

Decision-Theoretic Quality Economics

Optimal decisions under uncertainty, not just detection

Move beyond "is the process in control?" to "what is the expected cost of each possible action?" Using Bayesian posterior probabilities combined with loss functions (cost of false alarm, cost of missed defect, cost of investigation, cost of adjustment), compute the optimal decision at each moment. This is the mathematical formalization of quality economics — Taguchi's loss function meets Bayesian decision theory. The system doesn't just flag problems; it recommends the economically optimal response.

BenefitsOptimizes decisions, not just detection. Speaks management's language (cost, risk, ROI). Differentiates dramatically from statistical-only tools. Can be the basis for automated process control logic.
RisksRequires cost data that many companies don't have. Loss function specification is non-trivial. Over-optimization can create brittleness. Complex to explain and validate. Academic concept, limited practitioner awareness.
Effort: High Lock-in: ★★★★☆ SEO: Niche

The Three Principles

Every method in this report aligns with Svend's statistical philosophy:

1. Estimation over testing. Effect sizes and intervals, not p-values. (New Statistics, Bayesian credible intervals, conformal prediction intervals)

2. Evidence accumulates. Bayesian updating and anytime-valid inference, not fixed-sample rituals. (Bayesian Sigma, Sequential DOE, E-values, prior knowledge transfer)

3. Uncertainty is first-class. Every output has a calibrated uncertainty measure. (GP prediction intervals, conformal coverage, Bayesian posteriors, probabilistic monitoring)

Competitors cannot adopt this philosophy without abandoning their installed base. Svend has no legacy to protect.
