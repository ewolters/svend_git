01 — Strategic Thesis

Why Individual Plans, Why These Markets, Why Now

The US quality management market is dominated by enterprise procurement cycles — Minitab is embedded in IT-approved vendor lists, Six Sigma certification curricula, and corporate budgets. Displacing it requires selling to committees. That fight takes years and millions in sales infrastructure.

SE Asia and India have no such inertia. These markets are building quality infrastructure, not replacing it. The individual practitioner — the Green Belt at a pharmaceutical exporter in Hyderabad, the process engineer at a Samsung supplier in Bac Ninh, the quality lead at an automotive parts shop in Rayong — is making their own tool decisions.

Core Thesis

Svend doesn't compete for enterprise budgets. Svend competes for the individual practitioner's loyalty — arriving as their first real quality workbench, at a price point that fits their personal budget, through the training ecosystem that certifies them. When they advance into leadership, Svend goes with them. The enterprise sale happens later, organically, from the inside.

02 — Target User Profiles

Who We're Reaching and What They Need

Primary — The Certification Student

Who: Green Belt or Black Belt candidate currently in training. Age 24-35. Paying for their own certification (₹15K-80K in India, $200-$1,500 in SEA). Currently using Excel templates, pirated Minitab, or JASP because the school doesn't provide software.

Need: A tool to complete their certification project and that they'll actually continue using afterward. Needs to produce professional charts and reports that impress their instructor and, more importantly, their employer.

Trigger: Enrollment in a Lean Six Sigma training program. This is the exact moment they're looking for a tool.

Secondary — The Local Supplier Quality Engineer

Who: Quality or process engineer at a Tier 2/3 supplier to a multinational. Their customer (Samsung, Toyota, Bosch, J&J) demands quality data — SPC charts, capability studies, CAPA documentation. Their company won't buy Minitab. They're doing it in Excel.

Need: SPC, capability analysis, and root cause investigation that produces export-grade documentation. Must look professional when shared with the MNC customer quality team.

Trigger: Customer audit. Supplier quality requirement. FDA/ISO certification push.

Tertiary — The Consulting Trainer

Who: Independent or small-firm LSS consultant/trainer operating across India or SE Asia. Teaches Green Belt / Black Belt programs. Currently teaching Minitab but students can't afford it, or teaching Excel which limits what they can demonstrate.

Need: A software platform to build their training curriculum around. One that their students can actually afford to use after the course ends.

Trigger: Student complaints about Minitab cost. Competitive pressure from trainers using free tools. Desire to differentiate their program.

05 — Pricing Strategy for These Markets

Regional Pricing Architecture

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) matters. $29/month is affordable for an Indian quality engineer but still a considered purchase. For a Vietnamese factory worker, it's significant. The pricing needs to reflect this without cannibalizing US/EU revenue.

Recommended Approach: Regional Pricing via Geo-IP

Do not create separate "India pricing" pages. Instead, use geo-IP detection on the pricing page to show localized pricing automatically. Stripe supports this natively. The user sees their local currency and a PPP-adjusted price.

Technical Considerations

Internet Reliability
Svend is a web app — this is an advantage (no installation, works on any OS including Linux) but also a dependency. Internet in SE Asian industrial zones can be spotty. Consider: progressive web app features that cache the interface, clear "saving..." indicators, and graceful offline handling for data entry. Not a blocker for launch, but important for retention.
Localization Roadmap
Phase 1: English only. This covers India (professional English is universal in quality roles), Philippines (English-first), Malaysia (English widely used professionally). Professional users in Vietnam, Thailand, and Indonesia typically have working English for technical contexts. Phase 2 (if traction warrants): Hindi UI, Vietnamese UI, Thai UI — in that order based on market size and English proficiency gaps. Never localize documentation before UI — the UI is what users interact with daily.
Support Model
Async-first, documentation-heavy. You're a solo founder in SC. India is 10.5 hours ahead. Vietnam is 12 hours ahead. Live support isn't feasible or necessary. Invest instead in: comprehensive help docs, YouTube tutorials (double as marketing AND support), a community forum or Discord where users help each other, and email support with 24-hour response SLA. The training partners also serve as de facto support — their students ask them first.

08 — Honest Risk Assessment

What Could Go Wrong

Risk: Price = Low Trust Signal

In some markets, too-cheap software raises suspicion. "If it's this cheap, is it real?" Mitigate: never compete on price alone. Lead with the Insight Spine narrative, the RCA Challenge, the connected workflow. Price is the door opener. Capability is the closer.

Risk: Payment Friction in Vietnam/Indonesia

Stripe's coverage in Vietnam is limited. If Vietnam shows strong organic interest but low conversion, payment processing may be the bottleneck — not product-market fit. Mitigation: monitor sign-up-to-payment conversion by country. If Vietnam drops off, integrate VNPay or offer annual pricing via bank transfer.

Risk: Support Overwhelm

Success creates support load. If 100 Indian users sign up in a month, that's potentially 100 onboarding questions in a timezone where you're asleep. Mitigation: invest in documentation FIRST. Make the first-run experience self-guided. Training partners absorb first-line support for their students.

Risk: Copy Competition

If Svend proves the "affordable Minitab alternative" positioning works, local competitors may emerge. Mitigation: the Insight Spine is the moat. Cloning SPC is easy. Cloning the connected workflow from VSM through RCA Challenge through hoshin is not. Stay ahead on the spine, not the individual tools.

Risk: Founder Bandwidth

This plan is designed for a solo founder, but it's still a lot. The most important thing: do Channels 1 and 2 before anything else. LinkedIn content + training partner outreach. These have the highest leverage-to-effort ratio. YouTube, community seeding, and paid can come later. Don't try to do everything in Week 1.
