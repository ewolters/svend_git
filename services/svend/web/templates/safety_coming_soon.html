The Central Thesis

From Hazard to Risk — The FMEA Transformation

A hazard is a condition or behavior observed in the field. It becomes a risk when probability is introduced through FMEA scoring (Severity × Occurrence × Detection = RPN). The FMEA is the single management tool where this transformation occurs and where risk control decisions are made.

Every safety program that fails does so because it separates observation from action. Clipboards go into filing cabinets. Spreadsheets collect dust. The gap between "we saw it" and "we fixed it" is where injuries happen. HIRARC closes that gap by routing every observation through a single risk engine — the FMEA — where hazards are scored, prioritized, controlled, and verified in one living document.

System Architecture

Three-Stage Architecture: HI → FMEA → Verified Controls

The FMEA sits at the center as the single source of truth for risk management. Hazard identification vectors feed in from the left. Verified risk controls flow out to the right. The Management Operating System governs the cadence.

The Frontier Card

The Frontier Card is the primary hazard identification instrument. It is scheduled and assigned — not voluntary, not ad hoc. This is a DuPont STOP principle: observation programs fail when participation is discretionary. Minimum 2 audits per supervisor per week. Area managers conduct minimum 1 per week personally.

Front — Safety Observations

20 Items Across 6 Categories

  1. Body Position & Ergonomics — Lifting technique, reaching, bending, repetitive motion, static postures
  2. PPE — Correct type, proper fit, worn consistently, condition, availability at point of use
  3. Housekeeping — Walking surfaces, egress paths, storage, spills, lighting, overhead clearance
  4. Energy Control & Guarding — LOTO compliance, machine guarding, interlock status, electrical panels, arc flash labels
  5. Chemical & Environmental — SDS availability, container labeling, ventilation, exposure controls, waste handling
  6. Tools & Equipment — Condition, right tool for the job, inspection tags, storage, modifications

At-risk findings are severity-tagged: C (Critical), H (High), M (Medium), L (Low).

Back — 5S Deficiency Tallies

25 Items Across 5 Pillars

  1. Sort (Seiri) — Unnecessary items present, red-tag candidates, obsolete materials, expired supplies
  2. Set in Order (Seiton) — Shadow boards incomplete, labeling missing, tool locations unmarked, no visual management
  3. Shine (Seiso) — Cleaning schedule not followed, equipment dirty, inspection points obscured, fluid leaks
  4. Standardize (Seiketsu) — Standards not posted, inconsistent methods, no visual work instructions, missing checklists
  5. Sustain (Shitsuke) — Previous audit items unresolved, audit schedule missed, standards not maintained, discipline gaps

Tallies aggregate across audits. After 10+ observations, stack-ranked by frequency into a Pareto chart for standardization priorities.

Operator Interaction — Required, Not Optional

3 Required Questions

Every Frontier audit requires direct conversation with an operator in or near the zone. This is the defining feature of the DuPont STOP methodology and it is non-negotiable.

  • Approach with respect — you are a guest in their workspace
  • Ask the three card questions at minimum; follow the operator's lead for deeper conversation
  • Capture the operator's key concern and any improvement idea on the card
  • Close the loop before leaving: tell them what you observed and what action you will take
  • Never use audit findings punitively. This is a systems tool, not a disciplinary tool.

Severity Classification — Card Tag to FMEA Mapping

Card Tag FMEA Severity Meaning Action Expectation
C — Critical 9–10 Imminent danger. Potential for fatality or permanent disability. Stop work immediately. Isolate hazard. Enter FMEA same day. Escalate to plant manager.
H — High 7–8 Injury likely without action. Lost time potential. Enter FMEA within 24 hours. Interim control required immediately. Review at next MDI huddle.
M — Medium 4–6 Potential for injury. First aid or restricted duty. Enter FMEA within 1 week. Address through standard risk control process.
L — Low 1–3 Minor housekeeping, nuisance, comfort. Enter FMEA at weekly review. Address through 5S standardization or administrative control.
Audit Methodology

The D–S–O–I–R Audit Cycle

DuPont's STOP methodology, adapted for the Frontier Card. Every audit follows five phases. Total time: 5–7 minutes including operator interaction. This is not a time-intensive activity — it is a discipline-intensive one.

D
Decide
Select your assigned frontier zone from the weekly schedule. Review: have I audited this zone before? What did I find last time? What hazard categories should I focus on?
S
Stop
Pause at the threshold of the zone. Observe for 60–90 seconds without entering. Note first impressions: odors, lighting, noise, visible clutter, traffic patterns, postures.
O
Observe
Enter the zone. Work systematically through the Frontier Card (front: safety, back: 5S). Look up, look down, look behind. Open cabinets. Check dates. Trace cable runs.
I
Interact
Engage the nearest operator. This is required, not optional. Use the three card questions. Listen more than you speak. Capture their key concern and any improvement idea.
R
Report
Complete the card. Tag at-risk findings with severity. Submit by end of shift. Close the loop: tell the operator what you observed and what you are going to do about it.
Risk Scoring

FMEA Scoring — S × O × D = RPN

Each factor is scored 1–10. The Risk Priority Number (RPN = S × O × D) ranges from 1 to 1,000. Higher RPN means greater risk and higher priority for control action.

Factor Scale What It Measures Source
Severity (S) 1–10 How severe is the consequence if this failure occurs? 1 = negligible, 10 = catastrophic injury or fatality. Card finding severity (C/H/M/L mapped to scale)
Occurrence (O) 1–10 How frequently does this hazard occur or is this condition present? 1 = extremely rare, 10 = almost certain. Frontier Card tallies, Pareto frequency data
Detection (D) 1–10 How likely are current controls to detect the hazard before harm? 1 = certain detection, 10 = no detection capability. Current control assessment during audit
RPN 1–1000 Risk Priority Number = S × O × D. Determines prioritization for risk control actions. Calculated
Risk Control

Hierarchy of Controls — E-S-E-A-P

Once an RPN is established, risk controls are selected using the Hierarchy of Controls, most effective first. After implementation, the FMEA is re-scored (revised S×O×D) to confirm RPN reduction.

1

Elimination

Remove the hazard entirely. The only control with zero residual risk.

Redesign process to remove the step. Eliminate the chemical. Remove the elevated work.
2

Substitution

Replace with something less hazardous.

Less toxic chemical. Lower-voltage equipment. Lighter materials to reduce lifting.
3

Engineering Controls

Physical changes that isolate people from hazards.

Machine guarding. Ventilation. Interlocks. Anti-fatigue mats. Ergonomic workstation redesign.
4

Administrative Controls

Procedures, training, scheduling that reduce exposure.

SOPs. Job rotation. Training. Signage. 5S standards. Visual management.
5

PPE

Last line of defense. Protects the individual, not the system.

Gloves, safety glasses, hearing protection, respiratory protection. Must be specified, available, and enforced.
Governance

Management Operating System (MOS)

The HIRARC program is governed through a tiered MOS that cascades from daily operational review to quarterly strategic planning. Each tier has a named owner, defined activities, and specific KPIs.

Daily MDI Huddle

Owner: Supervisor
  • New hazards from yesterday's cards reviewed
  • Open FMEA action status checked
  • RPN > threshold items escalated
  • Today's audit assignments confirmed
  • Yesterday's audits verified complete
KPIs: # new hazards entered, # open actions past due, audit completion (yesterday)

Weekly FMEA Review

Owner: Area Manager
  • Full FMEA walk-through
  • Revised RPNs validated
  • 5S Pareto updated
  • Next week's audit schedule published
  • Weekly completion rate reviewed
  • Low-severity batch items scored
KPIs: Audit completion rate (target 95%+), # RPNs reduced this week, # new risks scored, 5S Pareto top 3 deficiencies

Monthly Trend Analysis

Owner: Safety / OpEx Manager
  • FMEA trend analysis (RPN movement over time)
  • 5S Pareto shift review
  • Systemic pattern identification
  • Cross-feed effectiveness review
  • Operator feedback themes
KPIs: Avg RPN trend (should decline), # hazards identified (leading), # controls verified effective, 5S Pareto concentration shift

Quarterly Strategic Review

Owner: Plant Manager
  • Program effectiveness review
  • New HI vectors evaluated
  • Capital projects from top RPNs
  • Auditor calibration / retraining
  • Zone coverage gap analysis
KPIs: Total RPN reduction vs baseline, zone coverage % (all frontiers), incident rate correlation, audit quality score
