The Bullwhip Effect
Small demand changes at retail amplify exponentially upstream. A 10% increase at the register can become a 40% spike at the factory. Causes: batching orders, demand signal processing, lead time delays, and price fluctuations.
Taming the Whip
Countermeasures: share point-of-sale data upstream, reduce lead times (compress the chain), use smaller/more-frequent orders, implement VMI (vendor-managed inventory), or level production with heijunka. Try changing the ordering policy above to see the difference.

The Service Level Trade-Off
Safety stock buffers against demand and lead time uncertainty. Higher service levels require exponentially more safety stock. At 99.5% the Z-score is 2.58 — nearly double the 90% (Z=1.28) level. Holding cost scales linearly; stockout cost hits in discrete, painful events.
Why Stockouts Still Happen
The formula assumes normally distributed demand and lead time. In reality: demand can cluster, suppliers face correlated delays, and compound uncertainty exceeds the model. The formula gives a target service level, not a guarantee. This simulator shows you the gap.

Why Level Production?
Batched production creates WIP mountains between changeovers. The first customer for product B waits until the entire A batch finishes. Leveling spreads changeovers across the day, reducing max WIP and average lead time — even if total changeover time increases.
The Changeover Trade-off
Leveled production requires more changeovers. This only works when changeover times are short (SMED). The economic crossover point: level when changeover cost < WIP holding cost. Try adjusting the changeover time above to find your break-even.

SMED in 3 Steps
1. Separate: Identify which tasks require the machine to be stopped (internal) vs those that can be done while running (external). 2. Convert: Move internal tasks to external where possible. 3. Streamline: Reduce time on remaining internal tasks through parallel work or quick-connect fixtures.
Why Single-Minute?
Shigeo Shingo's goal: every changeover under 10 minutes (single digit). Toyota achieved this on 1,000-ton stamping presses — from 4 hours to 3 minutes. The key insight: most changeover time is wasted on tasks that could happen before or after the machine stops.

Why Monte Carlo for FMEA?
Traditional RPN uses point estimates (S=7, O=3, D=4 = 84). But what if Severity is really 6-8? And Occurrence could be 2-5? Monte Carlo samples thousands of scenarios from these ranges, revealing the true risk distribution. A "safe" RPN of 84 might exceed 100 in 30% of scenarios.
Compounding Risk
Individual failure modes may look safe, but system-level risk compounds. If you have 10 modes each with 5% chance of exceeding threshold, the probability that at least one exceeds it is 1 − 0.9510 = 40%. This simulation makes that compounding visible.
